    Across Los Angeles, immersive spaces—from a panorama theatre to a time-bending retail space and disco bar—share a design language in multisensory storytelling to transport visitors to seemingly impossible locations and eras. Through the interactions of sounds, smells and textures, they ground fantastical encounters in embodied experiences.

    Amid cozy cafes and bookshops in Echo Park, a window display encases a brilliant orange terrain that vividly resists the ordinary. The scene belongs to Time Travel Mart, a storefront doubling as a speculative outpost stocked with dinosaur eggs, robot milk and Barbarian repellent.

    A vibrant storefront teasing at the oddities packed inside. (Photos taken by Jasmine Kwok)

    Oh, the things you may find at Time Travel Mart!

    Justin Stucey, designer of “Martian Landscape,” describes the exhibit as a “place that either you have been or that you are currently in.” From the sidewalk, the window teases an interior that invites perplexity and play.

    “To be immersed in one’s imagination, and to be immersed in one’s physicality is true,” Stucey says.

    Justin Stucey uses photography to document his ephemeral art pieces.(Photo provided by Justin Stucey)

    The transition from peering through glass to interacting with the products explores how movement through space can give imagined worlds weight and credibility.

    The compact space is filled with portals. Fridges open to books written by students across Los Angeles. A “Wormhole Text Messaging Center” reveals the store’s deeper purpose: a front for 826LA, a nonprofit uplifting young writers.

    In the back of Time Travel Mart, an entryway unfolds into a completely different world. (Video taken by Jasmine Kwok)

    826LA’s creative engagement specialist, Wilson Swain, began volunteering for the organization in 2010—or, according to Time Travel Mart, “since the beginning of time.” He says Time Travel Mart’s biggest initiative is to get people to ask “what’s going on here?”

    “It feels like a magical space, but it’s also so real,” Swain says. “Our space feels kind of familiar, so people aren’t afraid to enter.”

    Ordinary objects are designed as relics of the past or innovations for the future. Coffee becomes “fuel for the future. Cold beverages are “Time-Freezy Hyper Slushes” providing a taste blast from the past.

    Time Travel Mart has its own spin to the popular convenience store slushies. Drink this to freeze time! (Photo taken by Jasmine Kwok)

    “There’s a way of looking at normal objects around you and seeing it in a new way,” Swain says. “We want to make people excited to see the world that immediately exists around them.”

    Time Travel Mart suggests that portals exist everywhere—in windows, doorways and objects—but audiences have to be open to noticing them.

    Time Travel Mart in Echo Park is packed with useful artifiacts from the past, present and future. (Photos taken by Jasmine Kwok)

    Using cinematic storytelling techniques, the Velaslavasay Panorama communicates an interruption of the everyday. Like lights dimming before a movie, the panorama theatre’s narrow hallways are indicators of entering another place.

    “Playing with that ritual is a huge part of what we do,” Velas says. “All these elements are working together to create this true experience. Some of the little bits are a bit below the surface, so they’re easy to ignore, but they’re so impactful."

    Peer inside the Union Florist Shop, where strange things are taking place (Photo from the Velaslavasay Panorama)

    In 2020, the Velaslavasay Panorama sent invitations to the “Union Square Florist Shop,” an alluring storefront where passerby could peer into the daily workings of a floral service.

    Inside, a vignette came to life: ballroom music played, mysterious figures appeared through the window. A woman answered a rotary phone while watering plants; other workers arranged botanical displays.

    Strange happenings going on inside the Union Florist Shop. It's almost as if confusion is the entire point... (Video provided by Jennifer Jameson Merchant)

    “This was just something you could walk by accidentally,” Velas says. “This is a unique type of encounter because it’s tied to my day-to-day experience in a way I didn't plan for.” Intermingling everyday life with theatrics, Velas set the stage for people who just so happened to be in the right place at the right time. As more people gathered, the line between audience and participant blurred.

    “Sometimes these things have been purposefully created by humans, and sometimes they’re accidental happenings,” Velas says. “If you walk by a pile of discarded objects on a curb and look at it in a certain light, it could look like a sculpture.”

    For “Martian Landscape,” Stucey repurposed debris into an otherworldly terrain by coating discarded items in vibrant orange. The result was magnetic—viewers gravitated towards a shop they never planned to visit.

    “This idea of ephemeral happenings is very much embedded in this story of an adventure to Mars,” Stucey says. The installation reframes materials of everyday life as something transportive and intergalactic.

    These immersive environments aren’t always sought-out destinations. They can appear as chance encounters, transforming routines into something entirely new or fantastical.

    Down a dark corridor in Pico Boulevard, the world of “Brassroots District L.A. ‘74,” hums with neon lights, reverberating bass and thick incense. The immersive theatre and live concert experience pulls audiences into a 1970s discotheque.

    The Transformation Station subtly primes audiences to time travel by seamlessly integrating elements from the 1970s into the everyday world. (Photo provided by Brassroots District)

    Chris Porter, a writer for “Brassroots District,” discusses crafting a “transformation station” for participants to seamlessly enter another decade. In what Porter describes as a “nebulous space,” a pre-show patio party invites participants to interact with characters before the show officially begins.

    These exchanges are emotional time machines, grounding audiences in the resemblance of another decade before they’re thrust into it. At Brassroots District, people can feel, navigate, and relive history. These theatrical sleights of hand empower audiences to feel a sense of agency and direct influence over the story.

    “The big thing is eye contact—I’m talking to you,” Porter says. “People want that connection. Something about simply taking someone’s hand is so simple but powerful.”

    Chris Porter in front of the Brassroots District sign—the only opportunity in the experience where audience members can use their phones. (Photo provided by Chris Porter)

    Physical engagement isn't limited to direct human contact. Objects, environments and technologies can also evoke a tactile presence.

    Daniel Pillis, an assistant professor of virtual production at Emerson and affiliate researcher at MIT, examines how immersive design can connect people to places, people and times that are gone.

    A dining room in Pillis' installation of his grandmother's home (Photo provided by Daniel Pillis).

    In 2013, he developed a year-long experiment of “performing” life inside an installation version of his grandmother’s home. “It was an attempt at creating her conscious experience, her memories, her identity as a physical space that others could explore through technology, objects and media that comprised her life,” he explains. Even without her ever being there, Pillis felt he was living alongside her. The installation became a form of time travel—a space to inhabit a memory.

    Unlike traditional museum exhibitions, immersive environments like Brassroots District and Pillis’ work invite exploration over passive observation. Audiences are embodying the story. “We often interface with these places through a tour,” Pillis says. “It doesn’t feel like life. You need to be able to touch things to feel like it’s real.”

    By choreographing intimate, individualized moments, like a drifting scent or a secret conversation no one else hears, these environments transport someone to a place that no longer exists: a childhood kitchen, a relative's home or another era altogether.

    Peer into the peephole of the "Nova Tuskhut" to catch a glimpse of a man waiting in his rocking chair. (Video taken by Jasmine Kwok)

    At the Velaslavasay Panorama, “The Nova Tuskhut” recreates an Arctic cabin with aged wood, hunting tools and worn books. Rather than relying on contextual plaques, the walk-in archive captures a moment suspended in time, with memories “etched within its walls.”

    “It might not be 30 degrees below outside, but being in a small hut that smells of old wood and sitting on this small cot is a much more physical experience than reading in a textbook,” Velas says.

    In late 2025, “The Nova Tuskhut” became home to “The Man in the Tuskhut.” In a 20-minute experimental immersive experience, participants share a personal conversation with a man living in solitude at the edge of the world.

    “There’s an extremely special novelty of being able to interact in real time with an animatronic man,” Velas says. “Prior to this manifestation, each of these worlds had their own internal logics—and then the collision of these logics created another one.” The unique combination of the environment, animatronic and visitor generates an experience that feels unexpectedly personal and nothing short of magic.

    After a one-on-one interaction with the man, visitors can eat peanuts in the sunny Frontier Saloon while learning more about the "Man in the Tuskhut." (Photo taken by Jasmine Kwok)

    One of the first signs visitors encounter at the Velaslavasay Panorama: a teaser into the theatre's playful experimentations with time. (Photo by Jasmine Kwok)

    Some places carry an indescribable warmth—a feeling of being somewhere new yet oddly familiar. They retain traces of human presence in frayed edges, layered textures and subtle grooves.

    Travel across history with the Velaslavasay Panorama—a space that composites past, present and future.

    The Velaslavasay Panorama embodies this feeling, which Velas calls a product of “unrestored restoration:” the process of reimagining spaces and objects to make them appear lived-in rather than unrealistically pristine.

    “When visitors experience our marquee, they may think they’re finding a building stuck in time, but it’s really a hybrid of architectural details and inventions,” she says. The panorama theatre is its own living character, reflective of each building it has inhabited, the people who’ve entered and the places they represent.

    In 2004, the Velaslavasay Panorama moved into the Union Theatre in West Adams. After receiving funds for a marquee redesign, Velas preserved original elements while adding features from the panorama’s previous location—like the standout neon orange marquee—to give the building “a sense of Velaslavasay.”

    The theatre is surrounded by invisible histories. Even as exhibitions transport visitors to distant geographies, the Velaslavasay Panorama always considers the context of Los Angeles.

    Peering through a suitcase peephole or at a glowing diorama, visitors will find homages to the early 20th century rail lines that once connected the theatre to the metro station. “Those actual systems aren’t here anymore, but they still affect the way we experience the city,” Velas says. “As much as many of the projects we do transport you, they’re also reflecting feelings about Los Angeles.”

    Rather than striving for strict historical precision, projects like “The Union Florist Shop” layer archival images, found histories and collective memory to project the possibilities of what a florist shop could look like. Seemingly disparate components—the flower advertisements, the neon marquee—created a distorted sense of being caught in between time.

    Photo from the Velaslavasay Panorama

    Pillis’ research also questions how people remember places. “When we deeply imagine a place, am I just imagining visuals or more so the ambience?” Pillis asks. “I think it’s the feel of a certain collage of days, the temperature, the scent, the way the cloth felt, or the way the light bounced around.” Pillis suggests this sensory accumulation is what makes a memory feel real.

    Snapshots of a night spent dancing in the 1970s. (Photos courtesy of Brassroots District)

    The emphasis on atmosphere and lived-in detail carries into Brassroots District.

    Situated in Jewel’s Catch One— the oldest Black woman-owned disco in Los Angeles in the early 1970s—Brassroots District builds a world extending the club’s rich history.

    According to the Los Angeles Conservancy, Catch One was one of few nightclubs offering a safe space for Black, Latino and LGBTQ+ communities. Story beats in the show mirror the values of the dance bar.

    Porter credits the sounds and tactile ambience as the narrative engine. The concert’s lively soundtrack bridges a fictional plot with the backstory of Jewel’s Catch One and Sly and the Family Stone—a multiracial and multigender band whose music commentated on unity through upheaval.

    These messages of togetherness and authenticity are integral to Brassroots District. “The theme is already written in the space itself,” Porter says.

    Home to legacy venues and cinematic histories, Los Angeles is an ideal backdrop for time travel. “It’s sunny all year round, the seasons don’t really exist—it does feel like it’s a little outside of time,” Swain says.

    Flashing forward from the 1970s to the 2100s, Time Travel Mart’s space positions student writing with “time travel sickness” pills and fortune dice rollers. 826’s writing centers around the country—from pirate supply shops to an alien supermarket—share key characteristics: hidden doors and passageways that become transitions connecting past, present and future.

    “We want people to be intrigued by the space up front to get excited about the work in the back,” Swain says.

    Overlapping student writing with humorous representations of the time-warped artifacts, the convenience store collages the everyday with the otherworldly to create a dimensional space where time feels oddly fluid.

    Experiential design thrives in the “in-betweens.” The panorama combines three-dimensional faux terrain and figurines with a two-dimensional painted landscape to create an illusion of depth. Today, emerging technologies like virtual reality mix digital and analog mediums to make virtual spaces feel tangible.

    The "Shengjing Panorama" is filled with intricate details. In front of the painted city, each figurine reveals small narratives and new discoveries. (Photo taken by Jasmine Kwok)

    “When we’re in this in-between realm, we wonder which things we can rely on or take as truthful reality,” Velas says. These liminal environments can foster more opportunities for “serendipitous” encounters in daily life.

    Pillis says memory has been compressed into endless scrolling, replacing the act of flipping through dusty photo albums or rereading letters. “We have an effulgence of these partial experiences related to memory,” says Pillis. “You’re getting the bare bones symbol of someone existing through an image or text.”

    Audience members and characters stand in a tight circle for a moment of grounded presence before the show begins. (Photo provided by Brassroots District)

    Audience members and characters stand in a tight circle for a moment of grounded presence before the show begins. (Photo provided by Brassroots District)

    Immersive spaces can provide a counterbalance by offering a sense of physical presence impossible to scroll past. “People genuinely want something real,” says Porter. “Empathy is a muscle that we need to keep working. Stories allow us to do that.”

    For Velas, each ephemeral experience extends beyond the moment: it lingers in a memory, sensation or story that resurfaces.

    These environments, whether temporal or permanent, encourage people to reframe how they pay attention to their immediate surroundings. The magic is not the thrill of time travel or entering someplace entirely new but in all the invisible details—the sounds, textures and smells—that cling to the space and layer the everyday with moments of enchantment.

    Creative Vision:

    I developed this piece with the hope that this website can act as another portal to visit someplace new. This is a space that holds fragments of memories and collages the tiny moments that made each featured immersive environment wonderfully unique. Each person I spoke to offered such insightful perspectives on what it means to place someone inside a story. Even though each creative had their own approach, there seemed to be a shared design language used to invite wonder and play.

    Through visuals, sound, interaction and storytelling, this website looks to create a sense of movement, as if you're stepping through these places with me. Rather than treating documentation as static, I want to explore how digital spaces can reconstruct the feeling of being present, from the anticipation of walking into an unfamiliar world to the nostalgia of entering a new place that reminds us of home.

    For me, this capstone allowed me to appreciate how imagination, history, and spatial design intersect to create enchantment in our everyday world. Dark corridors in a certain light look like portals. Unassuming objects can turn into artifacts from the future. A flickering sign or hidden corner can transport someone into a different reality. These "in-between" places, such as the Velaslavasay Panorama, Time Travel Mart and Brassroots District, exist all around us, specially designed for serendipitous encounters.

    I hope visitors of this site wander, explore and follow their curiosity. Sometimes, just one sound, texture or interaction can momentarily whisk us away to a place or memory. Magic exists everywhere!

    -Jasmine Kwok
